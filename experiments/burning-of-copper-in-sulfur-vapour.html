Put sulfur powder (or pieces of sulfur) into a test-tube. Approximately 4/5 of the test-tube should be filled with sulfur. 2. Set the test-tube in a clamp at an angel. 3. Heat the test tube with a burner. At first sulfur melts, and then starts to boil. Heat the whole test-tube to vaporize sulfur which condensed on the wall of the tube. There should be a blue flame of the sulfur vapour near the open end of the tube. 4. At the same time heat a copper wire until it glows red and put in the test-tube. After a few seconds the copper wire burns in the sulfur vapour. safety_description: | This experiment must be performed in a well ventilated room or in a fume hood. Wear eye protection. Avoid inhaling the sulfur vapours. There is a serious fire risk - a fire extinguisher should be ready. Make sure there is nothing flammable nearby. science_background: | Copper doesn't burn in air or in oxygen, but it can burn in sulfur vapours. Heating the copper wire and an intensive stream of the sulfur vapours are needed to initiate this reaction. A black copper sulfide (Cu2S) is produced in this reaction.
